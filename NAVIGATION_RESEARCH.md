# AI Directory Navigation Research

This note summarizes navigation patterns observed from popular AI tool directories in May 2026. The goal is not to copy any one site, but to make the left navigation in this project easier for everyday users.

## Sites Reviewed

- Futurepedia: tools, resources, tutorials, automations, popular tools, recently added tools, business-oriented categories.
- FutureTools: tools, AI news, newly added tools, glossary, newsletter, submit a tool.
- Toolify: free tools, new AIs, most saved, most used, apps, browser extensions, GPTs, models, rankings, prompts.
- TopAI.tools: all tools, free tools, top 100, categories, use cases, playbooks, deals, search.
- aitools.fyi: pricing filters, productivity, image generation, web apps, marketing, analytics, education.
- AI Tool Hunt: discover, categories, trending, community, daily updated tools.
- Dang.ai: new, featured, top, deals, categories, affiliate programs, business/productivity/writing/development tags.
- Insidr.ai: AI for business, marketing, social media, content creation, SEO, ecommerce, free tools, tool reviews.
- OpenTools: tools, experts, knowledge hub, industry hub, resources, trending tools, guides.
- AI Tools Explorer: agents, audio, chatbots, data analytics, detectors, development, education, social media, marketing, productivity, research, translation, writing, industry.

## Takeaways

- Search is central, but navigation still matters for users who cannot name the tool they need.
- Popular directories combine multiple dimensions: category, task, pricing, popularity, content resources, and submission paths.
- The strongest UX pattern is task-first: "What do you need AI to do?" before technical labels.
- Content sections such as guides, playbooks, reviews, and comparisons help reduce list fatigue.
- Pricing/free filters are useful, but should not replace use-case navigation.
- Footer and disclosure pages matter for trust, especially when the site may include ads or affiliate links.

## Applied To This Project

The left navigation now prioritizes:

- Common entry points: scenarios, guides, comparisons.
- Real user contexts: student, office worker, creator, developer.
- Task filters: writing, PPT, translation, visual design, coding, data.
- Capability groups: content production, visual creativity, development and data.

This keeps the site useful for everyday users while preserving direct filtering for people who already know the category they want.
